Engagement surveys that actually move the needle.

By Dr. Aftab Ahmed · 09 Dec 2024
← Back to all insights

Every HR team runs an engagement survey. Most don't know what to do with the results. The questionnaire goes out, a 74% response rate comes in, scores get reported to the board, a few posters appear in the break room, and nothing changes. The tool isn't broken — the discipline around it is.

The five disciplines

  1. Ask fewer, better questions. Twelve well-crafted questions beat sixty lazy ones. The goal is signal, not coverage.
  2. Segment the results by manager, not by department. Engagement lives at the team level. Departmental averages hide it.
  3. Commit to action before you ask. Decide in advance which kinds of findings you will act on, and with what budget.
  4. Feed results back within two weeks. Delay kills credibility. The survey asks employees to talk; the speed of the reply tells them whether you listened.
  5. Close the loop publicly. Publish what you heard, what you'll change, what you won't, and why.
“The survey doesn't fail at the questions. It fails at the silence after.”

The manager-level unit of analysis

The single most valuable move in engagement measurement is to report at the team-plus-manager level. It turns a vague organisational score into a specific management conversation: this manager has a problem the others don't. It makes action possible.

What good looks like

A healthy engagement programme runs two short surveys a year — 12 to 15 questions — plus a handful of pulse surveys on specific topics. Each cycle produces a short action plan, three to five items, tracked publicly through to completion. The board sees a trend, not a number.

◆ Key Takeaways
  • Ask 12 good questions, not 60 average ones.
  • Report at the team-plus-manager level.
  • Commit to action before you ask.
  • Feed results back within two weeks, every time.
  • Close the loop publicly.

Engagement is not a score. It is a conversation. Run the conversation well and the scores take care of themselves.
