Killing the annual review — what to replace it with.

By Dr. Aftab Ahmed · 18 Mar 2025
← Back to all insights

The annual review is on life support. Most UAE employers still run one, most managers hate it, and most employees leave the meeting less motivated than they went in. Replacing it well is harder than it looks. The trap is to swap one broken ritual for another.

Why the annual review fails

Three structural problems. First, twelve months is too long — feedback delivered in December about something that happened in February is useless. Second, the rating distribution becomes the conversation — managers argue about labels, not performance. Third, pay decisions and development conversations get fused, which corrupts both.

What to replace it with

A three-layer system that separates the jobs the annual review was trying to do:

  1. Continuous feedback — short, real-time, event-driven. Delivered in the week the work happened, not the quarter after.
  2. Quarterly OKRs — three to five objectives per person, clearly measurable, reviewed as a rhythm.
  3. Calibration, twice a year — managers meet in a structured session to compare ratings across peers, eliminate drift, surface hidden stars and quiet underperformers.

The annual review itself, if you keep it, becomes a summary artefact — a half-hour conversation that surfaces no new information because the previous twelve months have already been captured.

“Performance management isn't a form. It's a set of habits, stacked.”

The manager upgrade

None of this works without a manager upgrade. Most managers cannot run an OKR cycle or deliver continuous feedback without training. Invest in a six-month manager academy before the system goes live. Without it, you'll just have better-named theatre.

Pay, separated

Decouple pay and performance timing. Pay decisions once a year, structured, pegged to band and market. Performance conversations year-round. When the two get fused, people stop telling the truth.

◆ Key Takeaways
  • The annual review fails because it tries to do three jobs at once.
  • Replace it with continuous feedback, quarterly OKRs, and bi-annual calibration.
  • Invest in manager training before the system goes live.
  • Separate pay conversations from performance conversations.

Good performance management is invisible. It's a set of rhythms that run all year. When employees describe the system, they shouldn't mention the form — they should mention their manager.
